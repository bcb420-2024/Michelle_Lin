Study Background Information

The study chosen for this project is hPSC-derived Airway Organoids-based Screen Reveals the Role of HIF1/ Glycolysis Axis in SARS-CoV-2 Infection published in 2021 by Duan et al.(Duan et al. 2021)

The authors conducted a study in human pluripotent stem cell (hPSC) derived airway organoids - (hPSC-AO) where they studied the molecular mechanism behind SARS-Cov-2 infections and the differential expression of different components of the pathways when treated with GW6571.

The following is a summary of the study chosen for this assignment.

# get information for study

gse <- GEOquery::getGEO(GEOID, GSEMatrix = FALSE)
currentGPL <- names(GEOquery::GPLList(gse))[1]
currentGPLInfo <- GEOquery::Meta(GEOquery::getGEO(currentGPL))

GEO ID of data set: GSE160230

Organism: Homo sapiens

Submission Date: Oct 27 2020

Last Updated: Oct 01 2023

Brief Summary of data set: SARS-CoV-2, the virus causing recently pandemic, primarily infects the respiratory tract. There is an urgent need to develop platforms using disease relevant human cells to dissect the molecular mechanism regulating SARS-CoV-2 infection and perform drug screen. Here, we derived airway organoids from human pluripotent stem cells (hPSC-AO). The hPSC-AOs, particularly ciliated cells, express ACE2 and are permissive to SARS-CoV-2 infection. Using hPSC-AOs, we performed a high content screen and identified GW6471, which blocks SARS-CoV-2 infection. RNA-seq analysis suggested that GW6471 blocking SARS-CoV-2 infection by inhibiting HIF1, which is further validated by chemotin, another HIF1 inhibitor. Furthermore, metabolic profiling identified that the downregulation of glycolysis upon GW6471 treatment, which is further validated by RNA-seq. Finally, xanthohumol, a prenylated flavonoid suppressing fatty acid and cholesterol biosynthesis, blocks SARS-CoV-2 infection. Together, we applied the high content screen, RNA-seq and metabolic profiling to define the key role of HIF1-glycolysis axis in SARS-CoV-2 infection, which provides a target pathway for anti-viral drug development.

Platform: Illumina NovaSeq 6000 (Homo sapiens)

Platform Last Updated: Nov 05 2018

For this study, we will pay particular attention to the GSE160230_raw_counts.hPSC_AO.downsample.txt i*n particular.

There were three conditions observed in this study - 3 mock samples - untreated and uninfected, 3 control samples - untreated and infected and 3 GW_treated samples - treated and infected samples. Due to the difference in higher observed viral counts and hence, lower human gene counts in SARS-Cov2 infected samples, the raw counts in the uninfected samples (mock and treated) was down-sampled so that the median human gene count was comparable to that in the infected samples. (Duan et al. 2021)

There were found to be 33834 unique gene mappings and 45887 ensembl gene IDs mapped, indicating that several of the Ensembl gene IDs mapped to the same gene. Hence, next, we will be aggregating these rows.

# Aggregate duplicated genes

## find duplicated genes and unique genes
duplicatedGenes <- unique(matchObj$hgnc_symbol[duplicated(matchObj$hgnc_symbol)])
uniqueGenes <- unique(matchObj$hgnc_symbol)

## aggregation and remove un-named gene
matchObj <- matchObj[, -which(colnames(matchObj) == "ensembl_gene_id")]
aggmatchObj <- aggregate(.~hgnc_symbol, data = matchObj, FUN=sum)
aggmatchObj <- aggmatchObj[-c(aggmatchObj$hgnc_symbol == ""), ]

## save intermediary objects
saveRDS(aggmatchObj, "AggregatedDataset.RDS")

DATA CLEANING AND PRE-PROCESSING

Remove Low Expression Genes

# choose min number of samples
min_samples <- 3

aggMat <- as.matrix(aggmatchObj)
rownames(aggMat) <- aggMat[, 1]
aggMat <- aggMat[,-1]

aggMat <- trimws(aggMat)
write.csv(aggMat, "aggregated_matrix.csv")

for (ri in seq_along(rownames(aggMat))){
  for (cj in seq_along(colnames(aggMat))){
    aggMat[ri, cj] <- as.integer(aggMat[ri, cj])
  }
}

intMat <- matrix(as.integer(aggMat), nrow = nrow(aggMat), ncol = ncol(aggMat))
colnames(intMat) <- c("Mock1", "Mock2", "Mock3", "Control1", "Control2", "Control3", "GW-Treated1", "GW-Treated2", "GW-Treated3")
rownames(intMat) <- rownames(aggMat)

satisfied <- rowSums(edgeR::cpm(int_matrix) > 1) >= min_samples
cleanData <- intMat[satisfied, ]

write.csv(cleanData, "CleanDataset.csv")
saveRDS(cleanData, "CleanDataset.RDS")

From the above, we removed 1.9162^{4} genes due to low expression values.
